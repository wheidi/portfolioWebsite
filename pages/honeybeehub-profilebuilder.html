    Honeybee is a platform

    Honeybee is a platform that connects people to Research Studies in need of participants. Researchers post their research studies to the Study Hive, and users browse these studies and participate in them.

    My role in Honeybee

    At the time, I was Honeybee‚Äôs first and only UX Designer and Researcher. As such, I was responsible for leading each project from its varying levels of abstract ideation, to a concrete, implementable design. The Honeybee Profile Builder was one of these projects.

    Research

    Discovering the problem

    From a recent survey on user experience, we found that 30% of our users were unsatisfied with our website. A teammate interviewed a few of these users, while I examined their survey responses, and together we found that 49% of these unsatisfied users had difficulty finding research studies.

    From this research we noted that our target audience was mostly:

    • Extrinsically motivated - they participated to gain monetary compensation
    • Main behaviour was browsing our home page instead of searching for specific topics

    Addressing the solution in 2 parts

    From my market research, I found that a profile builder would fit the bill. I then divided the profile builder into 2 parts.

    Part 1: Planning the Questions and Answers

    Planning the Questions

    Honeybee is unique in that we not only need personal interest information, but also a lot of demographic information to adequately filter out research studies.

    I looked at existing studies on Honeybee, SickKids Children‚Äôs Hospital, research papers, and many other research study boards to see what criteria was often used to determine eligibility.

    Question flow

    Planning the Answers

    How can I include and present all possible answers without overwhelming the user?

    Since the questions are multiple choice, I needed to figure out all answer options for each question. I had 2 main considerations here.

    • Include a minimal number of answer options to not overwhelm the user
    • Be inclusive and make sure all individuals will feel represented, avoid the use of the answer option, ‚Äúother‚Äù

    Spotlight: Representing Physiological Health Information

    I looked at medical intake charts, personalized health care websites, surveys, and other resources for inclusivity to better understand the possible answers to include.

    After a brainstorming session with my manager, we decided on a ‚Äúbody‚Äù approach where we‚Äôd present each answer option as a specific part of the body, and cover the entire human body.

    Pros

    • Inclusive - this would include all physical health conditions
    • Minimal number of answer options and avoid overwhelming users

    Cons

    • Users may be looking for their specific condition name at first

    Part 2: Designing the Profile Builder

    Considering this, I decided on the following conditions for the design of the profile builder:

    üòé Questions are easy to answer

    We have 2 different question types. Some questions are straight forward whereas others require more thought. How do we make some questions go smoothly and quickly, and encourage more effort on others?

    Multiple Select Questions

    How can we encourage users to select more answers?

    Questions that involve multiple selections require more thought compared to single select questions. How do we encourage our users to look at more options and consider them?

    The inspiration

    I pulled ideas from Spotify, Dribbble, and Youtube. Here, the behaviour is likely to look for something relevant and select it. This is the exact motivation that we want answering our multi-select questions.

    Insights

    • Images invite the user to look and see, whereas a lot of text is often ignored
    • Image is usually looked at first to capture interest and identify what the piece of media is about
    • Text information/titles are usually looked at next to give further context
    • An exception: visual hierarchy can be established based on location of text in image, if text is near the top, may read it first

    The key was to encourage our users to spend more time looking at every option and selecting them, as opposed to beelining for the minimum needed and continuing on.

    Key Design Decisions

    • Square shapes with plentiful padding + simplistic icons to avoid looking overcrowded
    • Prototypical icons to help convey meaning and encourage users to process information with the least amount of effort
    • Text to help clarify the categories
    • Vibrant and consistent selection colors to elicit more positive affect when you select an option

    Singular Select Questions

    How to make these questions the easiest and fastest to answer?

    Key Design Decisions

    • Use common language that they would expect/are looking for
    • Simplistic design with no extra material to be processed
    • Text to help clarify the categories
    • Order responses such that the most common ones are at the top

    ‚è±Ô∏è Feels short and fast to complete

    Not only did we want our profile builder to be objectively as short as possible, but also to be perceived as such.

    Hot Keys

    I added a hot key option, where pressing space bar would function as if clicking the ‚ÄúNext‚Äù button

    The ‚ÄúNext‚Äù button would have a prompt beside it, telling users to ‚Äúpress spacebar‚Äù

    Pros

    • The entire survey could be done from one hand position, without moving either hand
    • Simplistic design with no extra material to be processed
    • The mouse would not need to travel long distances to the ‚ÄúNext‚Äù button, decreasing time and effort required

    Cons

    • In multiple select questions, users would see the prompt and eagerly go to the next question, selecting less answers than they otherwise would have

    To accommodate for this con, I decided to not display the ‚Äúpress spacebar‚Äù prompt in multiple select questions.

    Feelings of accomplishment throughout

    I broke up the profile builder into 3 sections, breaking one big task into 3 mini tasks.

    Key Design Decisions

    • The section with the least amount of questions is in the middle, making it very easy to complete up to section 2 / 3.
    • Users would be more likely to complete the Profile Builder, as they‚Äôve made sizeable progress (2 / 3 sections) and wouldn‚Äôt want to see it go to waste

    Visual progress that feels impactful

    Progress feels good! We wanted a progress bar that felt satisfying but was still informative of how much of the survey users had left.

    Option 1

    Pros

    • Progress bar is easy to notice and is at a standard location

    Cons

    • Confusing what the lighter orange color could represent
    • 3 separate sections are not clear
    • Unfinished sections span a large portion of the screen
    • User will likely notice the progress bar when it is not progressing (when they read the question/when eyes are at the top of the screen)

    Option 2

    Pros

    • Clearly see that there are 3 different sections
    • User is more likely to view the progress bar when it will be growing - when the user interacts with the ‚ÄúNext‚Äù button

    Cons

    • Confusing whether the circles are sections or questions themselves
    • Unfinished/irrelevant portions of progress bar still take up a lot of space

    Option 3

    Pros

    • 3 different sections are clearly indicated
    • Current section takes up the most space, incomplete sections do not look too long
    • Progress bar growth animation is bigger
    • User is more likely to view the progress bar when it will be growing - when the user interacts with the ‚ÄúNext‚Äù button

    Cons

    • Users may not initially know what each individual progress bar represents

    I decided on Option 3 for the Progress Bar, as the pros are relevant and outweigh the cons. It it likely also, that once a question is answered the meaning of the different progress bars will be apparent.

    üîé Transparency and comfort when giving personal information

    Many of the questions in the profile builder are going to ask about sensitive topics. Like how Cade‚Äôs mindset indicated, how would we comfort and relieve the feelings of risk that may come with divulging this information?

    General privacy policy statements at the beginning of a section

    Privacy policy statements are included before a section with sensitive questions. Users generally only seek this information once. If they are worried later about how their information is being used, repeating our privacy policy will likely not help. Instead we‚Äôd give more specific information on why questions are being asked.

    Insights

    • General Privacy Policy statement is presented once at the beginning of the section to address general worries

    Personalized statements for specific questions

    I added ‚Äúi‚Äù buttons beside sensitive questions to let users know how this specific information is used and why we are asking it, including the greater impact it has in research. Hopefully, to build some prosocial motivation and to introduce the capacity of our product as well.

    Pros

    • Info bubble appears beside sensitive questions to inform users how exactly that information will be used
    • Mentions its prosocial impact on the world to help introduce our product‚Äôs mission and build prosocial motivation

    The Final Product

    A profile builder for a personalized experience



    The final product is a profile builder that appears upon signup. It takes less than 3 minutes and collects a reasonable amount of information from the user. This information will be used to present research studies that the user is eligible for, and may be interested in.

    What does this solution do?

    • Personalized experience for the user
    • Easier for new and returning users to engage with Honeybee and participate in research studies
    • Slower onboarding time, but is projected to improve conversion KPIs

    Reflection

    Due to our limited time and resources, we could not conduct user testing on many of the design decisions. As such, many of my decisions were based on pre-existing research from resources I could find online, feedback from my work peers and manager, and my own experience both as a student in the research space and as an aspiring UX designer as well.

    If we could‚Äôve conducted user testing, then many of the design decisions would be more well informed, pain points would be more realistic and accurate, and we could truly get a picture of our users‚Äô needs as well. As it stands, I did my best to empathize with Cade and to understand his needs. Hopefully moving forward, once we get metrics on how our profile builder and KPIs are affected, we can improve and iterate as needed.

    Most Notable Feedback + Going Forward

    Telling a Story

    A piece of feedback that stood out to me was, "this should feel like it's telling a story". I hadn't realized it, but I was only barely grazing the surface of what a well crafted profile builder could do. Though an experience could feel good by optimizing the visuals and elements, how to marry all of these together and tell a story was truly the soul of the challenge. Structuring the questions, giving them a logical order, giving the graphics a logical order, wording them consistently, these all contributed to the overall story of the experience.

    I do believe a profile builder has a lot of opportunities to be fun and exciting. Moving forward I‚Äôd love to design a similar profile builder that draws on the elements of character building in many online games.

    This could be done simply through the progress indicator. Making a lightbulb light up, a flower bloom, or a slime blob grow into a personalized slime. The possibilities are endless!
