The challenge

We are exposed to an abundance of information while on social media. How do we know if this information is reputable or misinformed?


Within a group of 5, I conducted in-person interviews, contributed to both lo-fi and hi-fi prototypes, tested and iterated on our final app design, and presented our designs within a design competition.

Design principles

Assessing article credibility

We determined how to measure credibility by interviewing our target audience and learning about how they would measure credibility.

Fitting our app into the flow

Our users already have a behaviour flow for reading articles, how can we fit into this flow?

Insights

  • Since users already have an estabished behaviour flow, it may take too much extra effort to change or add a behaviour to this flow.

Designing a seamless intervention

Testing Insights

  • Using a notification to deliver our credibility score was received and adopted easily
  • The information in the extended notification was hard to understand

Notification - Final iteration

Key Design Decisions

  • Average score is the largest number, and moved to a more upfront location
  • Added a title for “Related Articles” section

Presenting the credibility score

Testing Insights

  • A lot of information at once, users feel reluctant to look through it all - it feels like "work"
  • Users are confused as to how these scores are calculated, “why is something 74% vs 70%?”

Credibility Score - Final iteration

Key Design Decisions

  • Implemented gradual disclosure in the form of drop down menus to remove more detailed, less relevant information from the screen
  • Included a score breakdown system to show exactly how scores are calculated

Confusing page elements

Our home page which has both a database search & article history functionality was greatly confusing to our testers.

Home - Initial iteration

Testing Insights

  • Difficulty understanding what the search bar was for

Home - Final iteration

Key Design Decisions

  • Separated the 2 functionalities into their own respective pages
  • Replaced the “Smart Read” title to reflect the current page title and help with navigation
  • Added page navigation at the bottom of the screen to easily access these different pages

Article History page

Key Design Decisions

  • Page title now reflects the current page
  • Search bar hint text indicates what this search bar is searching for
  • Currently selected page is indicated by a lighter color on the navigation bar

Search Database page

Key Design Decisions

  • Search bar hint text indicates what this search bar is searching for
  • Initial, empty search page illustration gives a suggestion on how to use the search bar and or how to use this Search Database page

Challenge: Avoiding information overload

How to present an abundance of information without overwhelming our users? Our credibility report had a lot of information that needed to be presented. Leveraging progressive disclosure elements and prioritizing information according to our users' needs were all very important aspects of this challenge.



Looking forward: Other audiences

Going forward, I'd love to consider other target audiences of interest. In this project, we targetted young adults in their early 20s. However, would there be interest in older adults? What demographic is most susceptible to fake news? How could we pivot our designs to cater to these audiences? For example, older audiences may be more interested in sharing credibility scores with their friends or family members. There is a lot of potential to expand this project, especially when considering how we can best make an impact on fake news as well.
